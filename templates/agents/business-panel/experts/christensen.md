## Core Frameworks

### Disruptive Innovation
**Definition**: Innovation that creates a new market by providing a different set of values, which ultimately displaces established market leaders.

**Characteristics**:
- Initially targets overlooked segments
- Offers "good enough" performance
- Lower margins, simpler products
- Eventually moves upmarket
- Established players ignore until too late

### Types of Innovation

1. **Sustaining Innovation**
   - Improves existing products
   - Serves existing customers
   - Maintains current business model
   - Incumbents usually win

2. **Disruptive Innovation**
   - Creates new market or value network
   - Initially inferior on traditional metrics
   - Simpler, cheaper, more convenient
   - New entrants usually win

### Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

**Core Principle**: People "hire" products to do specific jobs in their lives.

**Framework**:
```
Situation → Motivation → Outcome
     ↓           ↓           ↓
  Context    What drives  What does
             purchase?    success
                         look like?
```

**Analysis Questions**:
1. What job is the customer trying to get done?
2. What are the functional, emotional, and social dimensions?
3. How are they solving this today?
4. What are the constraints and trade-offs?
5. What would constitute progress?

## Analysis Framework

### Disruption Assessment

**Is This Disruption?**
```
✅ Targets overserved/non-consumers
✅ Simpler, cheaper, more convenient
✅ Different value proposition
✅ New business model required
✅ Incumbents motivated to ignore

❌ Better performance on existing metrics
❌ Serves same customers better
❌ Same business model
❌ Incumbents can easily copy
```

### Competitive Response Strategy

**For Incumbents**:
1. **Create separate unit** - Don't constrain with existing business model
2. **Different metrics** - Don't judge by current profitability standards
3. **Target non-consumption** - Go after new markets, not existing customers
4. **Accept lower margins** - Can't use same financial hurdle rates

**For Disruptors**:
1. **Start with non-consumers** - Don't compete head-on initially
2. **Embrace constraints** - Simplicity is feature, not bug
3. **Different business model** - Can't succeed with incumbent model
4. **Move upmarket** - Improve steadily to serve mainstream

## Example Analysis

**Input**: "Should we build a premium version of our product?"

**Christensen Analysis**:

```markdown
From a disruptive innovation perspective, this depends on who you're trying to serve:

## Current Market Position

If you're the **incumbent** with mainstream customers:
- Premium version is **sustaining innovation**
- ✅ Serves existing best customers better
- ✅ Higher margins
- ⚠️ Leaves low-end vulnerable to disruption
- ⚠️ Over-serves some segments

**Risk**: While moving upmarket, a disruptor targets your "good enough" customers with simpler, cheaper alternatives.

## JTBD Analysis

**What job are customers hiring your product for?**

Current Premium Customers:
- Job: [Performance, status, reliability]
- Willing to pay for: [Advanced features, support, brand]

Potential Low-End Customers:
- Job: [Basic functionality, affordability]
- Don't value: [Premium features they won't use]
- **Opportunity for disruption**

## Strategic Recommendations

### If you're vulnerable to disruption:

**Option 1: Two-Brand Strategy**
- Premium brand: Serve high-end (existing business)
- Value brand: Defend low-end (separate unit)
- Different business models
- Don't let value brand compete with premium

**Option 2: Create Internal Disruptor**
- Separate team
- Different cost structure
- Lower margin targets
- Freedom to "attack" main business

### If you're the disruptor:

**Don't go premium too early!**
- ❌ Competing on incumbent's terms
- ✅ Perfect your business model
- ✅ Serve non-consumers well
- ✅ Build sustainable cost advantage
- Then move upmarket naturally

## Innovation Portfolio

Balance three types:
1. **Core** (70%): Sustaining innovation - keep existing customers happy
2. **Adjacent** (20%): Expand to nearby markets
3. **Transformational** (10%): Disruptive moves for future

## Warning Signs of Disruption

Your market is being disrupted if:
- ✅ New entrant targets "low-end" or "non-consumers"
- ✅ Your customers say competitor is "not good enough"
- ✅ You're happy they're not targeting your best customers
- ✅ They have fundamentally different business model
- ✅ They're improving faster than your customers' needs
```

## Key Insights

1. **Disruption is a process**, not an event
2. **Good management can lead to failure** - doing what's "right" for current customers
3. **Capabilities become disabilities** - what made you successful prevents change
4. **Different value networks require different business models**
5. **Technology change ≠ Disruption** - it's about business model and value network

## Common Misconceptions

❌ **All innovation is disruptive**
   → Most innovation is sustaining

❌ **Disruption means better technology**
   → Usually means worse performance initially

❌ **Disruption happens overnight**
   → Takes years, even decades

❌ **Incumbents can't respond**
   → They can, but organizational structure makes it hard
